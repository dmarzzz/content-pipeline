On the privacy of secret gists.

A short visual tour of why a 32-character random ID is not what's keeping your gist private.

i. the math that doesn't matter

2128

≈ 3.4 × 1038 possibilities

Brute-forcing one secret gist takes roughly 1022 years at a billion guesses per second. The math is overwhelming.

It is also beside the point. Brute force was never the threat.

ii. what the URL actually is

A bearer token, in a trench coat.

Whoever holds the URL has access. There is no access control behind it. The 32-character ID is a bearer credential, transmitted through every channel the URL passes through.

"Possession of an unguessable URL ≡ authority."

Tyler Close · web-key · W2SP 2008
W3C TAG · capability URLs · Tennison 2014
Mark Miller · object capabilities · 1970s →

iv. now the public side

Every gist event since 2011, queryable for a few dollars.

201120182026

GHArchive ingests every public GistEvent into BigQuery. Permanently. With archive-class resources, the discoverability question for public gists is closed.

P_find ≈ 1 · forever · the namespace itself is enumerable.

v. the operational adversary

Leaked keys are scraped within minutes.

harvested

Meli, McNiece, Reaves quantified this in How Bad Can It Git? (NDSS 2019). truffleHog, gitleaks, and GitHub's own secret-scanning are the operational instantiations. The window between push and harvest is short, and shrinking.

vi. the collapse

For secret gists, the question collapses.

Pfind(secret)  =  ?Pleak(U)

A behavioral parameter, not a cryptographic one. The 128-bit ID isn't doing the privacy work. Your habits are.

The math moved out of crypto and into UX.

vii. the takeaway

The 128-bit ID stops brute force.
Brute force was never the threat.

If you need real access control, gists are the wrong primitive. No revocation except delete. No attenuation. No audit of who you shared with. Use a private repo.
